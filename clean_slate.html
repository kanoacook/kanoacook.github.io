The Problem

In America, one in two sexually active individuals under the age of 25 is expected to contract an STD -- 70% of which is asymptomatic!

  • The current process for STI detection takes 2-5 days to deliver results, which is anxiety inducing
  • Current costs for at-home etesting ranges from $50-$100
  • Typical point-of-care devices require invasive methods of sample extraction, and the overall process has a lack of privacy

    • Our Approach

    A low-cost, rapid, non-invasive microfluidic device that takes is able to inform users of potential STI/UTIs! The process is as follows:

    1. 25mL of non-first pass urine is deposited into the device.
    2. The device is placed on a plastic stand, and an app is launched.
    3. A series of LED lights is shown onto the filter for 10 minutes. This acts a thermocouple for replicating the DNA -- think PCR!
    4. A push notification will contain the results.

    Background

    It all started as a class project!

    3 classmates got together in a microfluidics class with the idea of creating a microfluidic device capable of performing PCR on a microfluidic device. It just so happened that our professor had already done this!

    With his help, we had the idea of using PCR to test for the presence of bacterial infection biomarkers (Chlamydia and UTIs); however, as an undergraduate team, we had a tough time finding a medical collaborator that would give us clinical urine samples. This is where we got creative!

    Since E.coli is the most prevalent bacterial strain in a urinary tract infection (UTI), we would validate our device against this before trying other diseases!

    The Device

    Introducing CleanSlate

    CleanSlate is a microfluidic device that filters a patient's urine, simultaneously lyses bacterial DNA and performs PCR by shining LED light onto a plasmnonic gold nanofilm, and then glows upon the presence of a bacterial biomarker. Make sense?

    TL;DR, you pee on the device, shine your phone flashlight for 10min, and it'll glow if you have an infection.

    Initially we started with PDMS on a silicon wafer for uniform fabrication, but we found that the channels were too narrow to process as much urine as desired.

    We conducted a live/dead stain cell viability assay to show that cells were actually lysed by our filter!

    And, this is the results of the DNA replication after a PCR cycle to show that we successfully replicated E.coli DNA and acted as a biomarker for UTIs!

    Learnings

    We quickly learned that having a biotechnology startup is a steep hurdle. R&D is expensive, no one trusts you with biohazards unless you can fully substantiate your claims in advance via prior research, and everyone will think you're crazy until proven otherwise! It was exhilarating. Since our PI was moving from Berkeley to Harvard, we would have needed a large cash injection if we wanted to keep pursuing this idea further, which was a huge hinderance. We had looked into the SBIR process for funding or splitting our team into tech-side and business-side, but ultimately we decided that we could hang our hats and say it was a fun ride since the prospects of life were sounding a lot more promising at such young ages.
